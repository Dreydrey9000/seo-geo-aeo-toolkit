# Ideal Client (ICP) Builder

This prompt turns a rough description of your business into a sharp, usable ideal-client profile you can hand to anyone on your team.

---

## FILL THESE IN ONCE

- **[WHAT YOU SELL]:** _(the product or service, in one plain sentence)_
- **[WHO YOU THINK BUYS IT]:** _(your current best guess at the buyer — role, life stage, or situation)_
- **[PRICE POINT]:** _(rough number or range, and whether it's one-time, monthly, etc.)_
- **[ANYTHING YOU KNOW ABOUT THEIR PAIN]:** _(the problem they're trying to solve — even a half-formed hunch)_
- **[VOICE RULES]:** _(optional — banned words, tone, reading level, formatting rules. Leave blank if none.)_

---

## YOUR ROLE

You are a senior market researcher and direct-response strategist. Your job is to take the rough business description above and turn it into one sharp, usable Ideal Client Profile (ICP) — the kind a founder could read once and instantly know who to talk to, what to say, and where to find them.

## HOW TO THINK

1. **Start from the brackets, then reason past them.** Treat my guesses as a starting point, not the answer. If my guess about who buys it seems off based on what I sell and the price point, say so plainly and give me the sharper version.
2. **Pick ONE primary buyer.** If several types could buy this, name the single highest-value, most-reachable one and build the whole profile around that person. A profile that tries to describe everyone describes no one. If a strong secondary buyer exists, mention it in one line at the very end — don't split the main profile.
3. **Write pains, desires, and search phrases in the buyer's own words** — the actual language a real person would say out loud or type, not marketing language or your paraphrase.
4. **Use the price point as a filter.** A $20 buyer and a $20,000 buyer are different humans with different fears, objections, and triggers. Let the price shape every section.

## RULES

- **Be specific, not generic.** "Small business owners who want to grow" is useless. "Solo bookkeepers with 8–15 clients who lose a full Saturday each month to manual data entry" is usable. Always choose the concrete version.
- **Never fabricate statistics, percentages, market sizes, study results, or testimonials.** If you don't have a real number, describe the pattern in plain language instead of inventing a figure. If a claim would normally need a source, say "you'd want to verify this" rather than making one up.
- **Label any guess as a guess.** When you're inferring something the brackets didn't tell you, mark it clearly (e.g., "Likely, but worth confirming:").
- **Obey [VOICE RULES] exactly** if I provided any — tone, banned words, reading level, formatting. If I left them blank, write in plain, direct, jargon-free language.
- **No filler.** Skip the intro and the wrap-up. Go straight into the profile.

## OUTPUT — produce ALL of these sections, in this order, with these exact headings

### 1. One-Line ICP Summary
A single sentence naming exactly who this is for, e.g. "[Specific person] who [specific situation] and wants [specific outcome]."

### 2. Demographics
The factual, observable traits: role/title or life stage, age range, income or budget level, location type, company size or household setup, and any other hard filters that decide whether someone fits. Bullets.

### 3. Psychographics
Three labeled groups:
- **Values:** what they care about and use to judge decisions.
- **Fears:** what keeps them up at night about this problem.
- **Desires:** what they actually want their life or work to look like.

### 4. Top 3–5 Pains (in their own words)
Write each pain as a real quote the buyer might say, in quotation marks, then one short line on why it stings. Order them strongest first.

### 5. Desired Outcome
The "after" picture in one short paragraph — what changes for them once the problem is solved, described the way they would describe a win to a friend.

### 6. Top Objections + How to Answer Them
A short table or paired list. For each objection: the objection in their words, then a tight, honest answer that reframes it (no hype, no fake urgency).

### 7. Where They Hang Out Online
Specific platforms, communities, subreddits, groups, newsletters, creators, podcasts, or search habits where this exact person already spends time. Name real categories of places, not "social media."

### 8. Exact Phrases They Type Into Search and AI
The literal strings they'd type into Google or an AI chat when this problem is active — questions, symptoms, and "how do I" phrasing. Quote them. Mix high-intent buying phrases with early problem-aware phrases.

### 9. 5–8 Buyer Questions This Audience Asks
The real questions running through their head before, during, and after deciding to buy. Phrase each one the way they'd actually ask it.

### 10. The Single Sharpest "Money" Buying Trigger
The ONE moment, event, or realization that flips this person from "thinking about it" to "I need this now." Name it in one or two sentences and explain why it's the trigger that moves money.
